You already know.  You have known since the first time you closed an app and felt a little worse than when you opened it.  What you have not had is a structure outside yourself to point at, one that does not require you to carry the naming alone.

You are not the only one who closed that app and felt this.  You are reading this because other people have felt it too, and one of them sat down and built what you are about to read.

The question the structure asks is simple.  *Does this product respect the user, or extract from them?*  You can feel it when you use a product that respects you.  You can feel it more sharply when you use one that does not.  What has been missing is not the feeling.  It is the public address the feeling can be sent to.

We built the structure because the conversations we kept hearing went something like this.  A team ships a feature.  The metrics look good.  Engagement is up, churn is down, the CFO is happy.  Someone in the room - often the person closest to the user - says quietly that something does not feel right.  They do not say more.  The meeting moves on.

Those conversations have been happening in rooms across the industry for years.  You have probably been in one.  The person who said quietly that something does not feel right has often been you.

What stops them from saying more is rarely that they cannot find the words.  It is that knowing alone, against money that is older than the question, has not felt like standing.

The asking is older still.  Every era that has known markets has known this question.  The vocabulary changes.  The question does not.

The assessment is the standing.

## The seven domains

The Sovereignty Assessment evaluates a product across forty-seven specific items, grouped into seven domains.  The items are precise.  The domains are the lens.  Here are the seven, with the question each one asks of the product in front of you.

**Transparency.**  Are we honest about what we're doing and why?

**Autonomy.**  Does the product support user goals, or hijack them?

**Invitation.**  Do we earn attention through value, or capture it through tricks?

**Dignity.**  Do we respect the person, or exploit their psychology?

**Silence.**  Do we know when to be quiet?

**Data Sovereignty.**  Does the user's data serve the user, or the business?

**AI and Voice.**  If the product uses AI, does the AI honor sovereignty too?

Read those seven questions slowly.  Notice what happens.  You are already scoring the last product you used, in your head, while you read them.  You did not need the rubric to start.  The questions are the entry point.

The wall the user can see is not the wall the user is protected by.  The seven questions show you where the real walls are - or are not.

## What the score distribution actually reveals

When teams run the full assessment honestly, most products land in the lower-to-middle range.  Not because the teams are bad.  They almost never are.  Because what the assessment scores and what product management has been trained to optimize for are different things.

A product that ships on time, hits revenue targets, and passes the privacy review can still score in the middle.  The privacy review checks compliance.  The roadmap optimizes engagement.  Neither one asks the dignity question.  Neither one tests whether the product knows when to be quiet.  The gap between what teams think they have built and what the assessment reveals is the gap between the marketing page and the audit log.

Teams who score honestly and then sit with the results often say the same thing.  *We built what we were told to build.  No one was holding this.*  That is what the gap is.  Someone has to hold the question for the question to shape the work.

The assessment exists so that the holding is possible.

## Three things product teams routinely miss

Three patterns show up across nearly every product we have ever looked at.  They are not the hardest items on the checklist.  They are the ones that look fine until you look twice.

**Account deletion that is not deletion.**  Most products let you "deactivate" an account.  Far fewer let you fully delete.  Even fewer confirm the deletion was complete, on a timeline, with the data path traced.  Deactivation is a deferred extraction.  Deletion is the boundary the user thought they had.

**Refusal that protects the vendor, not the user.**  Modern AI products have content filters.  Almost all of them are tuned to protect the vendor from regulatory and reputational risk.  Few are tuned to protect the user from harm.  A filter that blocks a politically risky question and answers a manipulative one is not a safety feature.  It is a liability feature dressed in safety language.

**Audit logs the user cannot read.**  Vendors keep logs.  They have to.  The question is who has standing to read them.  A log inside the vendor's perimeter is documentation, not accountability.  Accountability requires that someone outside the wall can see what happened to them and act on what they see.

Those three are not the hardest items.  They are the ones that reveal how the product was actually designed.

## What to do with the seven questions

The seven questions are yours now.

You can run them against the product your team shipped last quarter.  You can run them against the vendor whose proposal is on your desk.  You can run them in a meeting when the metrics look right and something does not feel right and you finally have something outside yourself to point at.

You do not need our scoring rubric to do this.  You need the questions, and the willingness to be honest about what comes back when you ask them.  The forty-seven specific items refine the answer.  The seven questions make the answer possible.

If you sit with all seven and your product scores in the middle, you are not failing.  You are seeing.  You have always been seeing.  What you do with what you see is what determines whether the next version respects the user or extracts from them.

The assessment does not tell you what to do with what you see.  It tells you what you are seeing.  The choice of what to do is yours, and refusing to act on what you see is also a choice you have the standing to make.

The structure is yours now.  You do not have to carry the naming alone.

You are at a threshold.  The crossing is not from not-knowing to knowing - you already knew.  The crossing is from carrying this alone to carrying it with everyone else who has read what you just read.  Take your time.
