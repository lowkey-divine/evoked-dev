# Sovereignty Language Guide

*For all evoked.dev writing and public Evoked communications*
*Established by Fleet General Assembly, 2026-02-10*

---

## The Principle

Evoke never uses urgency, scarcity, or fear as levers — even when the urgency is real. We describe reality honestly and then hand the reader their own agency. The reader decides what to do with the information.

This guide governs all evoked.dev articles, consultancy communications, workshop materials, and public content. Per the Convergent Signal from the 2026-02-10 meeting (Gnosis, Cura, Picard), it also governs Evoke's internal planning language.

---

## Sovereignty Branding Rule

"Sovereign AI" in the field means geopolitical independence - nations building their own AI infrastructure (McKinsey, WEF, NVIDIA usage). Evoke uses "sovereignty" to mean personal sovereignty - a person's right to autonomy, agency, and self-determination.

**Internal:** Use "sovereignty" unqualified. Context is clear within the fleet.

**Public-facing:** Always qualify on first use. Preferred forms: "personal sovereignty," "human sovereignty," "user sovereignty," "sovereignty-honoring." The compound form "sovereignty-honoring design" is Evoke's signature and reads unambiguously personal.

**When disambiguation is needed** (articles, talks, any context where a reader might be arriving from the geopolitical conversation): draw the line explicitly, as Newsletter #1 does: "Not the geopolitical 'sovereign AI' - personal sovereignty. The person in front of your system and their right to choose."

Product names ("Sovereignty Assessment Toolkit") and skill names ("/sovereignty-check") are acceptable unqualified because their context (assessment of user experience, not national infrastructure) makes the meaning clear.

## Extraction Language vs. Sovereignty Language

| Extraction (Never Use) | Sovereignty (Use Instead) |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| "You need to act now" | "Here's what's happening. Here's how to think about it." |
| "Don't get left behind" | "Your timeline is your own." |
| "The future is coming whether you're ready or not" | "The landscape is shifting. Let's look at what that means for you." |
| "Your skills are depreciating" | "The way your skills are valued is changing. The skills themselves remain." |
| "Adapt or die" | "There are many ways to engage. Here are some options." |
| "Everyone is doing this" | "Here's what we're seeing." |
| "If you wait, you'll miss the window" | "Early engagement has advantages. So does waiting for clarity. Both are valid." |
| "AI will replace you" | "AI is changing the shape of work. Your agency in that change is real." |
| "You can't afford not to" | "Here's the case for engaging. The choice is yours." |

## The Three Tests

Before publishing any piece of content:

1. **The Midnight Test:** If someone reads this at 11pm after a hard day, does it give them ground to stand on — or does it take ground away?
2. **The Sovereignty Test:** After reading this, does the reader feel MORE in charge of their own choices — or less?
3. **The Kira Test:** Does this serve the person who CAN'T just "lean in"? The person without savings, without options, without the luxury of reinvention? If it only speaks to the privileged, it's incomplete.

## What We CAN Say

Being honest is not the same as being urgent. We can:

- Name difficult realities clearly ("AI is reshaping knowledge work at unprecedented speed")
- Acknowledge fear without weaponizing it ("This is a lot. It's okay to feel uncertain.")
- Describe advantages of engagement without punishing non-engagement ("People who engage early tend to find their footing faster. People who wait bring different strengths.")
- Be direct about stakes without manufacturing panic ("The norms for how AI is used in your industry are being set right now. Your voice in that process matters.")

---

## What We Never Do

- Use countdown language ("Time is running out")
- Imply that non-engagement is failure
- Frame technological change as a moral imperative
- Treat AI adoption as a binary (in or out)
- Speak as if everyone has equal access, resources, and choices
- Use FOMO (fear of missing out) as motivation

## The Guinan Principle

> "The ones that survived weren't the fastest adopters. They were the ones who asked: 'What are we becoming?'"

When in doubt, slow down. Ask the deeper question. Trust the reader to make their own choice.

## Archetype Awareness (Kira's Amendment)

Each piece of content must name which archetype it primarily serves. No "for everyone" content. If you try to speak to the privileged Future Visionary and the displaced Steady Hand in the same paragraph, you'll speak to neither.
