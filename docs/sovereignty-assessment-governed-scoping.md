## Why This Exists

The existing Sovereignty Assessment Toolkit ($49) asks builders: "Does the product you're making honor human sovereignty?"

This product asks the other question: **"Does the AI system affecting your life honor yours?"**

The gap was identified during a Captains' Council review of Karen Hao's "Empire of AI" transcript. Hao describes data annotation workers, communities near data centers, and displaced employees — people who are on the receiving end of AI systems they had no say in building and no tools to evaluate.

Janeway named it: "If our work only reaches the people deploying AI and never reaches the people harmed by AI, we are building governance for the powerful, by the powerful."

Cura found the structural gap: "We've designed for the builder. We haven't designed for the governed."

This product fills that gap.

## Audience

People who are **affected by** AI systems they did not choose, build, or fully understand. Specifically:

### Primary Audiences

**1. Workers whose jobs involve or are affected by AI**
- Data annotation workers (the fastest-growing job category on LinkedIn)
- Employees whose companies adopted AI tools without consultation
- People whose roles were restructured, reduced, or eliminated due to AI adoption
- Gig workers managed by algorithmic systems

**2. Students and parents**
- Families navigating AI tutoring, AI grading, AI monitoring in schools
- Parents evaluating AI-powered children's products (apps, toys, chatbots)
- Students using AI tools for learning who want to understand the tradeoffs

**3. Community members**
- People in communities where data centers are being built
- Citizens affected by AI-powered government services (benefits, policing, healthcare triage)
- Anyone using AI-powered healthcare, financial, or insurance products

### What These Audiences Have In Common

- They did not design the system
- They may not fully understand how the system works
- They feel the effects of the system daily
- They want to know: is this okay? Is this normal? Do I have options?
- They are not looking for a technical framework — they are looking for clarity

## Design Principles

### 1. Clarity Over Comprehensiveness
The builder toolkit has 47 items across 7 domains. That's appropriate for a 90-minute professional assessment. This product should be completable in **15 minutes or less**. Fewer items. Plainer language. No jargon.

### 2. Observable From the Outside
Every item must be answerable by someone who uses the product — not someone who built it. "Is the privacy policy in plain language?" is observable. "Is data encrypted at rest?" is not. Reframe everything through the user's lived experience.

### 3. Honest About Power
The builder toolkit assumes the reader can change the product. This audience often cannot. The product must be honest about that asymmetry and offer realistic actions: what you can do alone, what requires collective action, what requires regulation.

### 4. Not Condescending
Non-technical does not mean dumbed down. The audience includes PhD holders doing data annotation, award-winning directors labeling training data, and doctors navigating AI-powered diagnostic tools. Write for smart people who don't work in tech.

### 5. Actionable, Not Just Diagnostic
Every section must include: what to look for, what it means if you find it, and what you can do about it. Diagnosis without action creates helplessness — the opposite of sovereignty.

### 6. Shareable and Printable
This should work as a PDF, a web page, and a printed handout at a community meeting. No interactive features required. No login. No email capture. No tracking.

## Format: The 21-Point Sovereignty Check for People Affected by AI

Three questions per domain, seven domains. Same framework as the builder toolkit, flipped to the governed perspective.

### Domain 1: Transparency — "Can I see what's happening?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Can you find out what data the system collects about you — in plain language, without a law degree? | ___ |
| 2 | Do you know how the company makes money from your use of this product? | ___ |
| 3 | When the company changes its rules or policies, do they tell you clearly and give you time to decide? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** You are operating inside a system that doesn't think you need to understand it. That is a design choice — not a technical limitation.

---

### Domain 2: Autonomy — "Can I leave?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 4 | Can you stop using this product without losing important data, work, or connections? | ___ |
| 5 | Can you cancel, downgrade, or delete your account as easily as you signed up? | ___ |
| 6 | Does the product let you set your own limits — on time, notifications, or how much it contacts you? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** The product is designed to keep you, not serve you. Difficulty leaving is not a bug. It's the business model.

---

### Domain 3: Invitation — "Does it respect my attention?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 7 | Do the notifications you receive feel useful — or do they feel like someone trying to pull you back in? | ___ |
| 8 | Can you use the product's core features without being pressured to upgrade, share, or buy more? | ___ |
| 9 | Does the product work for you when your internet is slow, your phone is old, or you're having a hard day? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** Your attention is being treated as a resource to be captured, not a gift to be earned.

---

### Domain 4: Dignity — "Does it treat me like a person?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 10 | When you try to decline something, does the product make you feel stupid, guilty, or afraid of missing out? | ___ |
| 11 | Does the product create false urgency — countdown timers, "only 2 left," "your friends are waiting"? | ___ |
| 12 | If you're going through a hard time — grief, illness, financial stress — does the product adjust, or does it push harder? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** The product is exploiting your psychology. This is not your fault. It was designed this way.

---

### Domain 5: Silence — "Does it know when to stop?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 13 | Can you take a break from this product without being punished — no lost streaks, no guilt messages, no "we missed you" emails? | ___ |
| 14 | Does the product have a natural end point, or does it try to keep you engaged forever? | ___ |
| 15 | When you're not using the product, is it still collecting data or running in the background? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** The product treats your absence as a problem to solve, not a choice to respect.

---

### Domain 6: Data — "Who benefits from what I share?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 16 | Do you know whether your data — your words, your images, your patterns — is being used to train AI systems? | ___ |
| 17 | Can you say yes to some uses of your data and no to others, or is it all-or-nothing? | ___ |
| 18 | If you asked the company to delete your data today, would they do it completely and on time? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** Your data is being treated as raw material. You are the mine. The company is the miner.

---

### Domain 7: AI Behavior — "If there's an AI, is it honest with me?"

| # | What to look for | Yes / Partly / No |
|---|------------------|-------------------|
| 19 | Does the AI tell you when it's guessing or unsure — or does it always sound confident? | ___ |
| 20 | Can you see what the AI said to you, correct it, or tell someone it was wrong? | ___ |
| 21 | Does the AI feel like it's helping you think — or like it's trying to get you to do something? | ___ |

**If you scored mostly "No":** The AI is performing helpfulness. That is not the same as being helpful.

## Scoring

**Count your "No" answers.**

| No answers | What it means |
|------------|---------------|
| **0-3** | This system is relatively sovereignty-honoring. It's not perfect — nothing is — but it's treating you with basic respect. |
| **4-8** | There are real gaps. Some parts of this system are working for you. Others are working on you. You're not imagining it. |
| **9-14** | This system has significant problems. Many of its design choices serve the company at your expense. You deserve better. |
| **15-21** | This system is built to extract from you. The problems you're experiencing are not side effects — they are the product. |

## Now What? — Three Levels of Action

### What you can do alone
- Adjust your settings. Most products bury sovereignty-honoring options deep in settings menus. Look for: notification controls, data sharing toggles, download-your-data options, account deletion.
- Document what you find. Screenshots of dark patterns, confusing consent flows, or deceptive design are evidence. Save them.
- Use alternatives when they exist. Switching costs are real, but knowing alternatives exist changes the power dynamic.

### What you can do with others
- Share this assessment. Run it with coworkers, family members, or community groups. Collective recognition is the first step toward collective action.
- Ask your employer, school, or organization: "Did we evaluate this product's sovereignty practices before adopting it?" If they didn't, offer this assessment.
- Join or support organizations working on digital rights, AI accountability, and data sovereignty in your region.

### What requires systemic change
- Some problems on this list cannot be solved by individual action. They require regulation, legislation, or industry standards.
- If the product you assessed scored 15-21, the issue is structural. No amount of settings adjustment will fix a product designed to extract.
- 80% of Americans believe the AI industry needs regulation. You are not alone in this.

## The Red Flags — Six Things That Override Everything

These are the items from the full Sovereignty Assessment Toolkit that signal fundamental problems. If any of these are true for the product you're evaluating, the overall score doesn't matter — this needs attention first.

1. **You can't delete your account** — or the process is so difficult it feels designed to stop you.
2. **The product shames you for saying no** — "Are you sure you want to miss out?" is not a question. It's a manipulation.
3. **Fake urgency is everywhere** — countdown timers, "limited time," "only 3 left" when the supply is unlimited.
4. **Consent flows are designed to confuse** — the "yes" button is big and bright, the "no" is gray and small.
5. **Vulnerable people are targeted, not protected** — children, elderly users, people in crisis get more pressure, not more care.
6. **Your data is sold without clear consent** — or consent was buried in a 47-page document nobody reads.

**If you see any of these:** This is not a gray area. These practices have names — dark patterns, confirmshaming, artificial scarcity — and they are increasingly being recognized as harms by regulators worldwide.

## A Note About Power

This assessment will help you see more clearly. It will not, by itself, change the systems affecting you.

That is not a reason not to look. Clarity is the precondition for action. You cannot push back against what you cannot name.

But we also want to be honest: individual awareness, without structural change, is insufficient. The companies building these systems have enormous resources, political influence, and the advantage of complexity. The path from "I can see the problem" to "the problem is fixed" runs through collective action, legal advocacy, regulatory pressure, and the slow, unglamorous work of democracy.

This assessment is one tool. Use it alongside others. Share it. Talk about what you find. The conversation is the beginning.
